University of California, Los Angeles

Host 01 / ARPANET Birthplace

As the birthplace of the ARPANET, UCLA was selected to house the world's first Interface Message Processor (IMP) built by BBN. Under the leadership of Leonard Kleinrock, who pioneered the mathematical theory of packet switching, UCLA served as the Network Measurement Center.

Date OnlineSept 2, 1969
Hardware SystemSDS Sigma 7 / IMP #1

Stanford Research Institute (SRI)

Host 02 / NLS Research

The Stanford Research Institute received the second IMP and was the recipient of the famous "LO" message. SRI was home to Douglas Engelbart's visionary Augmentation Research Center (ARC).

University of Utah

Host 04 / Computer Graphics

Led by computer graphics pioneer Ivan Sutherland, Utah was the global epicenter of 3D visual computing and the birthplace of the famous Utah Teapot. By connecting their powerful DEC PDP-10 mainframe to the network, researchers nationwide could remotely access cutting-edge graphics software.

RAND Corporation

Node 07 / Military & Policy

Driven by Cold War nuclear threats, RAND researcher Paul Baran authored pioneering papers on distributed communications. He envisioned a survivable network without a central command hub, utilizing message blocks.

Massachusetts Institute of Technology

Host 15 / MULTICS Project

MIT brought immense computing weight to the ARPANET. It was the home base of J.C.R. Licklider, who first conceptualized the "Intergalactic Computer Network" while serving as an ARPA director.
