University of California, Los Angeles

Host 01 / ARPANET Birthplace

As the birthplace of the ARPANET, UCLA was selected to house the world's first Interface Message Processor (IMP) built by BBN. Under the leadership of Leonard Kleinrock, who pioneered the mathematical theory of packet switching, UCLA served as the Network Measurement Center.

On October 29, 1969, student programmer Charley Kline attempted to send the first host-to-host message to SRI. He typed "L", then "O". As he typed "G" for "LOGIN", the system crashed. Thus, the first message on the precursor to the internet was simply "LO".

Stanford Research Institute (SRI)

Host 02 / NLS Research

The Stanford Research Institute received the second IMP and was the recipient of the famous "LO" message. SRI was home to Douglas Engelbart's visionary Augmentation Research Center (ARC).

Having recently demonstrated the computer mouse, hypertext, and networked collaboration (NLS) in the 1968 "Mother of All Demos", SRI served as the Network Information Center (NIC) during the early ARPANET days, maintaining the official directory of all network nodes—essentially the early internet's yellow pages.

University of Utah

Host 04 / Computer Graphics

Including the University of Utah as the fourth ARPANET node was a strategic choice designed to prove the network could share specialized, heterogeneous computing resources.

Led by computer graphics pioneer Ivan Sutherland, Utah was the global epicenter of 3D visual computing and the birthplace of the famous Utah Teapot. By connecting their powerful DEC PDP-10 mainframe to the network, researchers nationwide could remotely access cutting-edge graphics software, proving the ARPANET was a distributed computing platform.

RAND Corporation

Node 07 / Military & Policy

Located in Santa Monica, the RAND Corporation represents the crucial military and strategic origins of the ARPANET.

Driven by Cold War nuclear threats in the early 1960s, RAND researcher Paul Baran authored pioneering papers on distributed communications. He envisioned a survivable network without a central command hub, utilizing message blocks. This meant the network could continue operating even if partial nodes were destroyed by a nuclear strike. RAND's connection physically linked this classified defense theory with ARPA's academic engineering.

Massachusetts Institute of Technology

Host 15 / MULTICS Project

As an East Coast academic powerhouse, MIT brought immense computing weight to the ARPANET. It was the home base of J.C.R. Licklider, who first conceptualized the "Intergalactic Computer Network" while serving as an ARPA director.

MIT's Project MAC utilized the ARPANET heavily to advance time-sharing operating system research, specifically the MULTICS project. MIT researchers played a central role in writing the early RFCs (Request for Comments) that defined the software protocol standards of the modern internet.
